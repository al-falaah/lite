Setting the Scene

1 Arabia Before Islam — A Mostly Oral World

When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was sent, Arabia was largely an oral culture. Writing was known, but rare — only a small number of people across the whole Arabian Peninsula could read or write.

Among those early literate Muslims were notable companions such as Abu Bakr, Umar ibn al-Khattab, Uthman ibn Affan, Ali ibn Abi Talib, Talha, Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan, Ubayy ibn Ka'ab, and Zayd ibn Thabit — both from Mecca and Medina.

When the Prophet ﷺ emigrated to Medina, he made a deliberate effort to spread literacy. He set a striking example: prisoners captured at the Battle of Badr could earn their freedom by teaching ten children of Medina how to read and write.[1] This one decision transformed literacy from a rarity into a growing asset in the early Muslim community.

The Prophet's Era ﷺ

2 Writing the Qur'an as It Was Revealed

Every single verse of the Qur'an was written down immediately as it was revealed, under the direct instruction of the Prophet ﷺ. He had a dedicated team of scribes — known as the Kuttab al-Wahy (كُتَّاب الوَحْي) — who recorded the revelation as it came.

⚠️ An Important Limitation At the end of the Prophet's life, the Qur'an was fully written down — but it was scattered across many different materials and in the hands of different companions. It had not yet been compiled into a single, organised book. This would come next.
Allah's Promise

3 Two Ways Allah Preserved the Qur'an

Allah promised to protect the Qur'an (Surah Al-Hijr 15:9). He did this through two complementary and interlocking methods — each supporting the other.

🧠

In Hearts — Memorisation

Thousands of companions memorised the entire Qur'an word-for-word as it was revealed. This living, breathing chain of human memory continued unbroken across every generation to this day. A memoriser is called a Hafiz.

✍️

In Writing — Scribes

The Prophet ﷺ appointed dedicated scribes to write every verse immediately upon revelation. These were the Kuttab al-Wahy (كُتَّاب الوَحْي). Their written records formed the foundation for later compilations of the Mushaf.

Neither method alone was sufficient — they checked and verified each other. This double-layered preservation is one of the Qur'an's most remarkable features in all of history.

First Compilation — approx. 11–13 AH / 632–634 CE

4 Collecting the Qur'an Under Abu Bakr ؓ

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, a grave crisis arose during the Ridda (apostasy) wars — many memorisers of the Qur'an were killed in a single battle. Umar ibn al-Khattab ؓ rushed to Abu Bakr and warned: if this continued, huge portions of the Qur'an might be lost forever.

Abu Bakr ؓ agreed and appointed Zayd ibn Thabit ؓ to lead the compilation. Zayd was the ideal choice: he had been a personal scribe of the Prophet, he had witnessed the final review (Al-'Ardah al-Akhiirah) of the Qur'an with the Prophet ﷺ himself, and he was known for his precise memory, trustworthiness, and scholarship.

"By Allah, if they had asked me to move mountains, that would not have been heavier upon me than what they have commanded me — to compile the Qur'an."

— Zayd ibn Thabit ؓ, describing the weight of the task

Zayd's rigorous standards for what he included:

  • ✓
    Confirmed as Qur'an only

    Nothing that was explanation, commentary, or personal note from companions was included — only the revealed text itself.

  • ✓
    Mass-transmitted (Mutawaatir (مُتَوَاتِر))

    A verse was only included if it was confirmed by multiple independent witnesses — not just one person's memory or note.

  • ✓
    Confirmed in the Final Review

    Only verses that were part of the Al-'Ardah al-Akhiirah — the Prophet's ﷺ final review of the Qur'an with Jibreel ﷺ — were included. This locked in the final order and content.

The completed manuscript was kept by Abu Bakr ؓ during his caliphate, then passed to Umar ؓ during his, and after Umar's death it was entrusted to his daughter Hafsa ؓ, the Mother of the Believers — until it was needed again.

Standardisation — approx. 25 AH / 645 CE

5 Standardising the Qur'an Under Uthman ؓ

Islam had spread rapidly. Muslims from different regions — Arabia, Persia, Syria, Iraq — were now reciting the Qur'an in slightly different ways. A brave soldier named Hudhayfah ibn al-Yaman ؓ noticed this alarming trend during campaigns in Armenia and Azerbaijan, and rushed back to Uthman ؓ with a warning.

"Save this nation before they fall into disagreement like the disagreement of the Jews and Christians!"

— Hudhayfah ibn al-Yaman ؓ to Caliph Uthman ؓ

Uthman ؓ convened the senior companions and, with their agreement, moved decisively. He recalled Hafsa's official manuscript, then appointed a committee of four trusted companions to copy it precisely:

✍️
From Medina

Zayd ibn Thabit — the veteran scribe of revelation who had led the first compilation

📖
From Quraysh (Mecca)

Abdullah ibn al-Zubayr, Sa'eed ibn al-Aas, and Abd al-Rahman ibn al-Harith ibn Hisham

The committee's rule: nothing was written unless it was first checked against all companions present in Medina and confirmed to be Qur'an — recited in the final review and affirmed by multiple witnesses.

Once the standardised copies (known as the Uthmani Mushafs) were complete, one was sent to each major city. Everything else — unofficial personal copies — was ordered to be destroyed. This bold act of unification ensured that, from that point forward, there was one universally agreed text.

📖 The "Imam Mushaf" Uthman kept one copy for himself in Medina. This is called the Mushaf al-Imam — the master copy, because all the other Uthmani copies were made from it first. Most scholars say six copies were distributed: to Basra, Kufa, Syria, Mecca, and two for Medina. Some say a copy also went to Yemen and Bahrain, making eight in total.
Oral Transmission

6 How the Qira'at (Recitations) Reached Us

The Qur'an was not only preserved in writing — it was (and still is) transmitted orally, mouth to ear, teacher to student, across every generation. The different Qira'at are authorised ways of reciting the Qur'an, all going back to the Prophet ﷺ himself.

Generation 1 — The Companions (Sahabah)
Thousands heard the Qur'an directly from the Prophet ﷺ
Companions spread across the Muslim world, each teaching what they had learned. Among the most prominent teachers: Ubayy ibn Ka'ab, Abdullah ibn Mas'ood, Abu Musa al-Ash'ari, Zayd ibn Thabit, and others from both the Muhajireen (emigrants) and Ansar (helpers).
Generation 2 — The Tabi'een (Successors)
Students of the Companions took up teaching in every city
In Medina, Mecca, Kufa, Basra, and Syria — scholars dedicated their lives to teaching the Qur'an. They became so expert and so widely followed that people flocked to them from distant lands. Each major city produced its own generation of Qur'an masters.
Settling of the 5 Main Cities
Each city's recitation became identified with a specific master reader
Gradually, the recitation of each city crystallised around a leading imam whose chain back to the Prophet was unimpeachable.
The Seven Readers — 3rd Century AH
Ibn Mujahid formalises the Seven Qira'at
The scholar Ibn Mujahid wrote the landmark book Al-Sab'a fil Qira'at ("The Seven in Recitations"), selecting seven outstanding imams — one from each major city — whose recitations were famous and universally accepted. This became the foundation of Qur'anic recitation studies.
City Leading Reader(s) Why They Stood Out
Medina Abu Ja'far & Nafi' Nafi' had a vast chain of teachers going back to the Companions
Mecca Ibn Kathir Studied under Mujahid — himself a student of Ibn Abbas ؓ, the Prophet's cousin
Kufa Asim, Hamza & Al-Kisa'i Three successive imams whose recitations became dominant in the east
Basra Abu Amr & Ya'qub al-Hadrami Well-known for precise, careful recitation
Syria (Sham) Ibn Amir al-Dimashqi The oldest of the Seven Readers with the highest chain of transmission back to the Prophet ﷺ
🔑 What Makes a Qira'ah Valid? A recitation is only accepted as authentic if it (1) matches the written Uthmani Mushaf, (2) is linguistically correct Arabic, and (3) has an unbroken chain of transmission back to the Prophet ﷺ through multiple narrators. All three conditions must be met.

What This All Means

The Qur'an is the
best-preserved text in human history.

No other book from the ancient world can claim what the Qur'an can: written records from the moment of revelation, verified against the memories of thousands, compiled within two years of the Prophet's ﷺ passing, standardised into a single text within a generation — and recited, without break, by millions of people in every century since.
